A pronounced fluctuation in BP,* i.e. BPV,# can leave patients at risk. No clinical diagnostic definitions or treatment goals have been widely adopted to measure BPV in clinical practice.1 The risk of CV events, particularly stroke, rises and coincides with an increase in BP levels in the first few hours of the morning (usually b‌etw‌een 6‌ and 1‌0 A‌M).2 The data correlating rise of BP and risk of CV events may help in better CVD management of your patients.
Hourly frequency of onset of CV events2
The link between the early morning BP surge and an increase in CV events is well established. Acute MI has been shown to be three times more common at 9‌ a‌m than at 11‌ p‌m and 44&zwnj;% of the ischaemic strokes occur in the morning.3,4
Adapted from Mead et al. 2008.
A higher morning BP surge has been associated with stroke risk and silent infarcts, independently of the mean ambulatory 24-hour BP5
While BP fluctuation throughout the day is normal, a pronounced fluctuation in BP can leave patients at risk. BPV is abnormal and can occur over short-term observational periods. In addition, it is difficult to measure in clinical practice, and no clear diagnostic definitions or treatment goals have been widely adopted.
